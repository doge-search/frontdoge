The California Institute of Technology (abbreviated Caltech) is a private doctorate-granting university located in Pasadena, California, United States. Although founded as a preparatory and vocational school by Amos G. Throop in 1891, the college attracted influential scientists such as George Ellery Hale, Arthur Amos Noyes, and Robert Andrews Millikan in the early 20th century. The vocational and preparatory schools were disbanded and spun off in 1910, and the college assumed its present name in 1921. In 1934, Caltech was elected to the Association of American Universities, and the antecedents of NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, which Caltech continues to manage and operate, were established between 1936 and 1943. The university is one among a small group of Institutes of Technology in the United States which tends to be primarily devoted to the instruction of technical arts and applied sciences.
Caltech has six academic divisions with strong emphasis on science and engineering, managing $332 million in 2011 in sponsored research. Its 124-acre (50 ha) primary campus is located approximately 11 mi (18 km) northeast of downtown Los Angeles. First-year students are required to live on campus, and 95% of undergraduates remain in the on-campus House System at Caltech. Although Caltech has a strong tradition of practical jokes and pranks, student life is governed by an honor code which allows faculty to assign take-home examinations. The Caltech Beavers compete in 13 intercollegiate sports in the NCAA Division III's Southern California Intercollegiate Athletic Conference.
Caltech is frequently cited as one of the world's best universities. Despite its small size, 33 Caltech alumni and faculty have won a total of 34 Nobel Prizes (Linus Pauling being the only individual in history to win two unshared prizes), 5 Fields Medalists, 6 Turing Award winners have been affiliated with the university, and 71 have won the United States National Medal of Science or Technology. There are 112 faculty members who have been elected to the United States National Academies. In addition, numerous faculty members are associated with the Howard Hughes Medical Institute as well as NASA. In a 2015 Pomona study, Caltech ranked number one for the percentage of its graduates that earn Phd after graduation.

I'll shed some more light from the Berkeley perspective as an EECS/Regents' graduate. Long answer impending...

Class sizes

It is certainly true that lower div courses and popular upper div ones can easily exceed 100 or 200 students. This is partly because of the large EECS + CS population but general classes in math, econ, psychology, etc. are going to be large. Meanwhile there are many upper divs that have between 20 to 80 students, and some graduate courses have under 20. There are also undergraduate courses with fewer than 20 students and graduate courses with more than 100, although the general trend is that more advanced and specialized courses have fewer students.

Ask yourself if you'd significantly benefit from in-class discussion and office hours with the professor. Small classes are the most beneficial when you are interested in the material and want to know more outside of the scope of the course.

Classes offered

Take a look at the draft schedule for 2012–2013: http://www.eecs.berkeley.edu/Sch.... It's always good to plan one semester in advance so you know when the really good or popular professors are teaching a class. There's lots of choice and freedom in EECS once you hit the upper division.

Core requirements & AP credits

Caltech is more focused on general engineering (which can ingrain some very good skills). At Berkeley there is a large amount of freedom in EECS especially. You can apply AP credit to several breadth courses and beyond that it's basically five EECS lower divs and five upper divs plus some miscellaneous requirements. A wider exposure to EECS, graduate work, other engineering courses, and cross-disciplinary studies are some of the directions you can invest in with your time.

Keep an eye on the AP credit guidelines because they can change in your favor. During my last semester AP US Government counted as a humanities course and I was able to take a graduate CS course in its place. My Student Affairs counselor asked, "How'd you find that? We didn't announce it so you guys would take your humanities." On the other hand I had AP credit for both French and Japanese that they wouldn't honor because engineering students can't earn humanities credit for language courses, which is one of the worst things about the College of Engineering. I was told that while several faculty members shared my views, the Undergraduate Study Committee voted that rule into play and it would require revisiting the issue at a future date. An unfortunate example of process by committee.

GPA

You will have to work hard but the actual numbers are more generous than what hearsay implies. The College of Engineering posted the average GPAs of engineers back in 2010:
Freshmen: 2.80
Sophomores: 3.16
Juniors: 3.17
Seniors: 3.25
Overall: 3.20

There are a few reasons why students are doing better than the grading guidelines might suggest (http://www.eecs.berkeley.edu/Pol... ):
These GPAs include more than EECS courses. The university as a whole grades more leniently.
Professors usually want to encourage students while being fair. They will often curve the class average closer to the high end on the guidelines. Many times they will exceed it.
There's a selection bias since many freshmen who did poorly are no longer represented in the sophomore and higher demographics.
Median and average GPAs are often very different since there are inevitably some people who get an F. A few 0.0s can lower the average enough for a professor to shift the rest of the curve up. There are many EECS courses in which 50% of the students receive some type of an A.

One of the downsides of Berkeley is that A+s count for 4.0 points. Some schools count them as 4.3. If you apply to law school, though, you'll get to count A+s as 4.333.

Statistically speaking again, I think you are an above-average admittee who is likely to be an above-average student. If schoolwork is one of your priorities you can do very well.

Cost

I don't know if a Caltech degree will pay off that $100,000 difference over the course of your lifetime, but a CS degree (plus lots of hard work) from either school will let you live comfortably.

Regents' Scholarship

Regents' scholars of my time are envious of the priority registration and other benefits of the scholarship that have improved recently. Priority registration is great for small classes with popular professors and will save you from signing up for 1.5x the classes you intend to take as you anxiously wait for the EECS department to gradually open up more seats and for your peers to pare down their course lists. (Protip: when you are on a waiting list, choose the discussion section with the fewest students like one in the early morning or a Friday. When there is an open seat for the main lecture, it is given to the first student with an open discussion section, not necessarily the student who was #1 on the waiting list.)

As for the other benefits, I went to maybe two or three of the dinner events and never met my faculty advisor. The scholarship didn't directly help with research opportunities, but I think that some of the characteristics that earned you it will help you with research as well.

Research

A student survey awhile ago showed that many students were having difficulty finding research. Meanwhile, all of my friends who wanted to try their hand at research were able to do so. Work hard, find projects you are interested in and potentially good at, and be proactive.

The proximity to LBNL isn't important unless you're interested in a specific project there. There are several research labs in Soda Hall; to name a few major ones, strong support from industry has helped fund Berkeley's RAD Lab (2005), Par Lab (2008), and AMP Lab (2011). Berkeley CS is very strong in research and industry collaboration, but you will need to seek out those opportunities on your own since the university's primary responsibility to you is an undergraduate education.

Internships and Jobs

Many companies visit Berkeley several times a year to recruit software engineers. Several of these are on the list of industry partners: http://www.eecs.berkeley.edu/IPR... and there are regular infosessions in the fall and spring during the recruiting season. Since it sounds like you are interested in industry, there are two points I want to emphasize:
Apply aggressively when you are a freshman. Once you get one big-name internship it becomes noticeably easier to get another. Support your resume with independent projects and project-oriented upper div courses.
Work every summer. This is perhaps the only time in your life you will be able to work at a different company every summer without any hard questions asked. Capitalize on this opportunity if you don't need to do summer school. You'll also come back a stronger programmer than before with 40+ hours a week under your belt.
Another local data point: all of my CS friends who wanted a full-time job were able to find one.

Double majors

The primary reason to do a double major is a strong interest in another subject. Most double majors don't do the MSE or NE programs since you can multi-major outside of the combos set up (for example, Jinghao Yan got degrees from three Colleges for EECS/Business/Economics). There's also the EECS Honors program if you have interests in another discipline but don't want to do a minor or major. EECS is one of the most flexible majors that can be completed with time to spare; the question is whether you want to dedicate that time to a second major, more breadth or depth in EECS, or something outside of school such as a startup or early graduation.

Master's

The 5th-year MS is a good program if you're confident you're not interested in a Ph.D. but want the extra year of advanced schooling. It helps a lot to have strong letters of recommendation from professors and ideally one who is willing to be your research advisor and wants to work with you. For most opportunities an MS degree on your resume can help direct attention away from your BS if you didn't do as well as you'd have liked during undergrad.

Rankings

Comparing #3/4 to #7/8 isn't that meaningful unless you're playing in a tournament. Your own ability and motivation plays a much larger role when the rankings are that close and you should pick the place that makes you more comfortable. What is it like to study computer science at Caltech?
Answer:
This question is perhaps more difficult to answer about CS than other scientific fields. It's much easier for someone to learn and practice on their own before college in CS than in chemistry, for example - you don't need a lab, you just need a computer and Internet access. Some CS majors began learning at a young age (I did), and some only came into the field in college or even later. I believe the experience in CS varies quite widely for this reason, so I'll just write about my experience studying CS as an undergraduate - what follows is based only on my thoughts and impressions.

Computer science was thought of as the "easy" major at Caltech. Aspiring chemical engineers told me that my life was easy because I didn't have to stay up all night studying arcane transport phenomena. At the quarterly career fairs, some recruiters would be bold enough to interrupt groups of students talking amongst themselves to ask if any of them were CS majors. (The event was commonly referred to as the "CS Fair" by undergrads.) The CS degree requirements allowed a lot of room for electives, and were considered relatively light in comparison to other degree requirements.

Despite all of this, I would not say that CS was "the easy major". All CS majors had to take introductory classes on programming, systems, complexity and algorithms theory, discrete mathematics, and scientific writing and presentation. A year-long project was also required; last year (2011-2012), project classes were offered in database design, VLSI design, networking and distributed systems, machine learning and computer vision, and graphics. There were plenty of electives as well, most of which extended from one of the project areas listed.

In keeping with the institute's general trend, the CS department had strong programs in more theoretical fields like complexity, algorithms, machine learning, and AI, but not in compilers, systems, and software engineering. For me, this was a disappointment. I was interested in infrastructure and systems, but Caltech offered only an intro systems class. An advanced operating systems class wasn't offered until the final term of my senior year, and even then it was a one-term class offered under the "special topics" label (to be fair, it was well-taught and rigorous, but one term just wasn't long enough to cover all of the relevant material). Caltech apparently had had a faculty member who specialized in systems, but he left the institute before my freshman year and his vacant position was never filled.

The teaching quality in the Caltech CS department was surprisingly good. Although the intro algorithms class was generally taught by professors who clearly focused on research rather than teaching, most of the other intro classes were well-taught. In fact, the intro complexity theory class was regarded as one of the best classes at Caltech even by non-CS majors. Teaching quality didn't decline in higher-level classes, though class sizes were smaller and classes were sometimes taught by TAs. Still, many students skipped lectures and instead read lecture notes or slides posted by instructors. There were about 25 students in the GPU programming class last year, but by the fourth week, only three of them regularly came to class.

I can't say too much about research at Caltech since I didn't do much of it when I was there. My research was a collaboration between the CS and geology departments to implement a specific form of geological modeling using GPU-based techniques for speed. It seemed to me that there were plenty of opportunities for research, but not many of them were pure CS; most opportunities were collaborations with other departments. Independent student projects could be counted for credit even if there weren't any faculty members specializing in the project area, but these projects were less likely to generate publications.

Caltech puts a heavy emphasis on research, but in CS, academia and industry aren't so different in terms of the projects undertaken. Internships were readily available (most easily via the career fair), and I spent my summers in industry. Eventually I was convinced not to go to grad school and instead to work at Quora, but that's a different story.
