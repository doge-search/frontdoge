编辑



The University of California, Berkeley (also referred to as Berkeley, UC Berkeley, California or simply Cal) is a public research university located in Berkeley, California. It is the flagship campus of the University of California system, one of three parts in the state's public higher education plan, which also includes the California State University system and the California Community Colleges System.
It is considered by the Times Higher Education World University Rankings as one of six university brands that lead in world reputation rankings in 2015 and is ranked third on the U.S. News' 2015 Best Global Universities rankings conducted in the U.S. and nearly 50 other countries. The Academic Ranking of World Universities (ARWU) also ranks the University of California, Berkeley fourth in the world overall, and first among public universities. It is broadly ranked first in science, third in engineering, and fifth in social sciences, with specific rankings of first in chemistry, first in physics, third in computer science, fourth in mathematics, and fourth in economics/business. The university is also well known for producing a high number of entrepreneurs.
Established in 1868 as the result of the merger of the private College of California and the public Agricultural, Mining, and Mechanical Arts College in Oakland, UC Berkeley is the oldest institution in the UC system and offers approximately 350 undergraduate and graduate degree programs in a wide range of disciplines. The University of California has been charged with providing both "classical" and "practical" education for the state's people. Cal co-manages three United States Department of Energy National Laboratories, including the Los Alamos National Laboratory, Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory and Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory for the U.S. Department of Energy.
Berkeley faculty, alumni, and researchers have won 72 Nobel Prizes (including 30 alumni Nobel laureates), 9 Wolf Prizes, 13 Fields Medals (including 3 alumni medalists), 22 Turing Awards, 45 MacArthur Fellowships, 20 Academy Awards, 14 Pulitzer Prizes and 105 Olympic gold medals (47 silver and 33 bronze). To date, along with Berkeley Lab, UC Berkeley scientists and researchers have discovered 16 chemical elements of the periodic table (californium, seaborgium, berkelium, einsteinium, fermium, lawrencium) - more than any other university in the world. Lawrence Livermore Lab also discovered or co-discovered six chemical elements (113 to 118). Berkeley is a founding member of the Association of American Universities and continues to have very high research activity with $730.7 million in research and development expenditures in the fiscal year ending June 30, 2014.

先要说明我对Berkeley是偏爱的。Berkeley是大众的，自由的，草根的。然而她又是权威的，顶级的，前沿的。某老师曾说过每天在学校里面走，一想到和自己擦身而过的人都可能是未来的诺贝尔奖获得者就莫名鼓舞。Berkeley与Stanford和那些整洁风格一致的校园最大的不同就是它的“杂乱“，但这也是它迷人的地方。历史的推移在这里留下了各种印记，从古老到新潮一步一景。藏在校园里还有很多小溪，石桥，只供有心人驻足。美的不张扬是我爱她的理由。
因为自己毕业于Berkeley，所以曾经作为地陪带很多朋友逛过这座有爱的校园和城市。下面是我一般推荐的路线，希望也能让你爱上她。建议你配合google map使用，这样路线会比较清楚。
Oxford St. 的星巴克
上学的时候天天从这里进入校园，所以就从这里开始吧。
West Gate （西大门）
上台阶，映入眼帘的是蜿蜒直上的进校”山路“。感受到扑面而来的智慧和激情了吗？
Eucalyptus Grove（桉树林）
深呼吸，这是桉树的清香。国内可不能经常碰到的。以前每天进入学校经过这里都会精神抖擞，心烦压力大的时候来这里减压，天然的熏香理疗。
Valley Life Sciences Bldg（生命科学楼）
里面有一个古生物博物馆。周末可能进不去，但是值得在外面守候一下等人进出时尾随。地下一层有一具巨大的恐龙骨架。相较之下Google's T-REX弱爆了吧！这是Berkeley合作发掘出土的霸王龙骨架复制品。
Free Speech Movement Cafe
作为美国精神的基础的言论自由是从Berkeley开始。这家小咖啡馆是专为纪念这一伟大运动而命名的。咖啡馆外有世界各地的报纸英文头版，每天更换。提醒学生们不要只读死书，不问窗外事。咖啡馆内有当年运动的照片和历史介绍，户外座位还可以遭遇不怕人的松鼠前来讨食。
California Hall
东亚研究系馆，以前东亚图书馆的前身。原来的图书馆在二层一个非常古老的阁楼里，每一层都堆满了书架，二层的地板还是毛玻璃的，每次去都有一种在书中隐居的感觉。如今不知还在不在？
Memorial Glade
每个美式校园都有的大草坪。多少个下午曾在这里躺着晒太阳，听钟声，看打着赤膊的帅哥玩飞盘，和穿着衣服的美女晒太阳。这里是照相的大俗地。捎上图书馆，和钟塔。

Question:

What reputation do Berkeley CS grads have among Stanford and MIT grads?
At the Undergrad level, at the grad level?
Answer:
Wow, there seems to be several answers here from people only a couple of years out of school, and while I guess their opinions might be an accurate reflection of those of their cohort, some of them are so stereotypically arrogant as to be embarrassing.

As a Cal grad who has worked in the valley, started a company, and who currently has MIT and Stanford alumni working for him, I would say that graduates of these schools share a mutual respect that is reflected in the fact that so many technology giants have founders or executives from both sides of the Bay. Steve Jobs' creativity needed Steve Wozniak's genius. Scott McNealy's vision was backed by Bill Joy's innovations. Larry and Sergei's great idea needed Eric's execution. Moore, Grove, Hewett, Packard...

Even Stanford's current president, John Hennessy, got famous (and rich) due to his collaboration with Dave Patterson at Berkeley. I got to shake Hennessy's hand at one of Patterson's retreats, and I can tell you the two are still good friends and collaborators.

From the couple dozen graduates from each school that I have worked with I can tell you that there is a stereotype that is somewhat accurate, that Stanford grads are more "entrepreneurial" and are often thinking of their next career jump, and Cal grads are more "geeky" and interested in technology for technology's sake. Still, it's a stereotype that breaks down pretty quickly when you deal with real individuals. From the small number of MIT grads I've worked with they seem to line up with Cal grads as geeky, although in the end CS/EE alumni from any of these schools are unbelievable nerds compared to the general population. :)
Answer:
I attended MIT for undergrad and Berkeley for MS and PhD, all in Computer Science. Based on my experience as a student and from TA-ing 2 undergraduate courses at Berkeley, my observation is that MIT has a higher concentration of talent than Berkeley at the undergraduate level. The distribution at MIT is more top-heavy than at Berkeley. Top performers overlap between the two schools, however. The distribution makes it statistically easier to stand out at Berkeley than at MIT.

Culturally, MIT and Berkeley CS departments are quite similar. MIT has better undergraduate hackers. Undergraduates at MIT are much, much more involved in doing research -- even research that has real impact. I've mentored many undergraduate and masters students at Berkeley and most of their contributions have been marginal. The workload at both schools is quite high. However, I found that at Berkeley, professors are a bit more lenient with grading than at MIT.

At the graduate level it's the same across all three. Berkeley is particularly good at systems. MIT is really good at combining systems and theory. Stanford is similar to MIT.

My impression from working with Stanford researchers and giving talks down there is that it's just a whole other world from MIT/Berkeley. I picked Berkeley over Stanford, largely because of the cultural overlap between MIT and Berkeley. The environment at Stanford feels more formal and there's a strong emphasis on entrepreneurship. MIT was also quite entrepreneurial but less so than Stanford. Berkeley was the most "academically pure" amongst the three -- although statistics show that many successful startups are started from Berkeley alums, it's less integrated into the culture of the department.

Last, I still think MIT is the mecca for most technical fields. Although MIT, Stanford, and Berkeley CS are all equally top-notch, I would bet that most people with offers for faculty positions from the three would pick MIT.

From the Stanford perspective, here are some lines of thinking:

Undergraduates
Because Berkeley is so much larger and has so many more students in general, the best CS undergraduates that you'll find are on par with people from private (and more prestigious) institutions like MIT and Stanford.
However, the mean and median are probably lower.
One factor that plays into this is that undergraduate admissions are more competitive at Stanford than Berkeley, since Berkeley's charter as a state school means that it largely serves the California population, whereas Stanford has more national and international prominence.
Another factor that may be important is that private schools like Stanford have large endowments and in general more resources to facilitate undergraduate education. The teaching staff : student ratio at Stanford is much better than at Berkeley, which enables closer feedback on code quality and style from an early stage. For example, for the CS106 and CS107 introductory classes, section leaders and TAs read over the code for every single programming assignment submitted, in order to give feedback; it's not just grading scripts running and spitting out scores.

Grad Students
Berkeley's EECS graduate program is extremely strong, one consequence of which is that many professors now at Stanford did their PhDs at Berkeley.
Since a person's PhD program depends much, much more on the adviser than the institution, there isn't much information in which institution someone chooses to attend.

Question:

Can I go to graduate school even if I don't want to pursue an academic career?
I am really interested about grad level courses especially in AI but I want to work in a company and not in academia. I have two years of professional experience (SE) and I will graduate in 1 year from a top notch Canadian University. What are my options? I am especially interested in Stanford University, what does it take to get in?
Answer:
There are so-called "terminal" masters programs for people who don't want to pursue PhDs. You'll get to do graduate courses, and sometimes a masters thesis, but you won't go on to get a PhD. These programs usually take two years and are great if you just want to go into industry afterwards. Compared to a PhD program, these are more focused on classes rather than on research.

The main disadvantage is that you will probably have to pay for this program yourself, whereas PhD programs tend to be funded.

Stanford has a CS Masters program like this. It's probably exactly what you're looking for. It's much less selective than their CS PhD program. In fact, based on hearsay and reputation, I don't think it's that selective in an absolute sense: not as selective as you'd expect Stanford to be.

You should note that some schools really de-emphasize their pure masters programs. Berkeley is a great example: it has a top-notch CS department, but you should probably avoid it if you just want a masters rather than doing a research-focused PhD. (In fact, I think they only added a separate CS masters track recently, although I'm not sure exactly how it works.)

I strongly suspect that six years of kicking butt as a professional in your field would do more to raise your income than a similar investment of effort in the PhD, and you'd be getting paid well along the way. One colleague tells new and prospective students "think of it as costing you a house" and I think that's about right.
