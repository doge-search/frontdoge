Virginia Tech

编辑



Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University, popularly known as Virginia Tech, is a public, land-grant, research university with a main campus in Blacksburg, Virginia, educational facilities in six regions statewide, and a study-abroad site in Switzerland. The commonwealth's third-largest university and leading research institution, Virginia Tech offers 225 undergraduate and graduate degree programs to some 30,600 students and manages a research portfolio of $513 million. The university fulfills its land-grant mission of transforming knowledge to practice through technological leadership and by fueling economic growth and job creation locally, regionally, and across Virginia.
The Virginia Tech Board of Visitors, which serves as the university's governing body, comprises 13 members who are appointed by the governor of Virginia; the president of the state's Board of Agriculture and Consumer Services serves ex-officio as the 14th voting member. Serving as non-voting representatives are the presidents of the university's Faculty Senate and Staff Senate, who serve ex-officio, and an undergraduate student and a graduate student selected through a competitive review process. The endowment is managed by the Virginia Tech Foundation, and as of 2015 topped $817 million.

Question:

What would be some really good reasons to join Virginia Tech for a masters program?
What would be some really good reasons to join Virginia Tech for a masters program?
Answer:
I am sure you'd have done your research about how good or bad the particular program you want to join is at VT, so keeping academics aside, as a Virginia Tech alumni who graduated with a Masters degree, I can tell you some "insider details" that might make you consider joining-

Blacksburg is a beautiful place. Yes, I mean it. Yes, it is a tiny college town in the middle of nowhere with the nearest big cities 3 or 4 hours drive away, but this actually works in your favor when you are a student at VT. The university campus and the town are very safe. Do not let the 16 April 2007 massacre affect you. It was a one-off unfortunate event. In my two years at VT, I never felt unsafe walking down empty roads at 2am to get grocery or being on a transit bus alone. After graduating I have lived in Washington DC, Dallas and San Francisco and when I look back, I am surprised to see the vast difference between life in big cities and Blacksburg. No homeless people wandering on the roads, no shady neighborhoods, no drug-addicts sleeping on the buses. Everything is student-friendly and easy going.




Beautiful campus. Kinda related with previous point, VT campus is gorgeous. Be it the characteristic "Hokie stone" buildings, the huge Drillfield in the center of the campus, the Duck Pond, the campus has a sense of serenity that allows you to focus on your studies, relax and de-stress yourself.

Value for money. VT is not one of the most expensive schools in the country. In addition, Blacksburg is a pretty cheap place to live. If you do not want to drive to school daily, you can use very good free Blacksburg Transit bus service and spend $0 on your travels. Rent for off-campus housing is also around half of what I paid in bigger cities after graduation.

Hokie sports! If you are into college football or sports in general, I don't even need to tell you how much fun being at VT will be. Football gamedays are just amazing at VT. The entire town transforms itself into a carnival, yes other universities also go mad on gamedays but the relative small size of Blacksburg makes the festivities look grander!

Well I'm sure you've decided by now but for the sake of answering and for anyone else that stumbles into this page, I'll expand on some points from previous posts from my experience as an undergrad:

- Yeah, the campus food is rated first in the nation in terms of college food. Bit of a subjective rating, your taste may not be the same as the people who rates these things. There are quite a few dining halls but of course there's a limit to how much you want to keep going to the same place to eat and off campus food is lacking in diversity.

- Blacksburg has some nice scenery around it. Good hiking trails and nature scene. Definitely in the middle of nowhere though and you will feel it during breaks when the students are gone. Due to it being a college town, it isn't very lively when the students are gone.

- Weather is nice in the fall and spring but winter feels like it will never end. The mountains around Tech make the weather a bit ADD at times so it's hard to predict. One day it might snow, the other day might be 80+ degrees.

- The campus is fairly large. Hokie stone beauty is in the eye of the beholder. Lots of people like it, a minority feel that it looks dreary and feels more depressing when it rains (also, puddles everywhere on the Drillfield!)

- Definitely cheap. Finding a place for <=$350/month shouldn't be too hard.

- Sports are something you have to attend at least once. Even if you're not into football like I am, there's a certain energy that you want to be a part of on game days.

- Students are mostly from Northern Virginia. It's just me saying this but they do come off as rather sheltered, especially during their first few years at Tech. Engineers have a bit of a reputation of complaining a lot about having "no lives" when they could just organize some free time into their schedule instead of using it to complain. They also have a tendency to look down on non-engineering classes/degrees but I think that isn't unique to Tech. As a master's student though, you'll have limited interaction with them and more interaction with people who care more about things others than a big paycheck (as you can tell I'm still a bit bitter over dealing with undergrad engineers).

- As a master's student...all these reasons are secondary. Don't make a decision soley based off of any of them. Look at the department and what faculty are up to in their research. Tech has many alumni and so look at where they have ended up. The strength of the program takes the most importance (but don't forget that the physical environment may affect your time in the program as well).

Question:

Where are the graduate classes for Computer Science held at Virginia Tech?
Is it in a particular building? Where is the CS dept? VT is a big place and I am looking at places to live on google map.
Answer:
I'm a former CS student from Virginia Tech, so I feel I can answer your questions.

For the most part, the courses are held in McBryde Hall, which is where the student CS lounge and office are located. Courses are held in other locations, such as Goodwin and Randolph Halls. The buildings mentioned are all located in the same general area of campus with an estimated five minute walk at most between McBryde and Goodwin. The Virginia Tech Time Table of Courses is open to the public, so you can do further research using that if you'd like.

If you're looking for places to live off campus, I suggest joining the Virginia Tech network on Facebook by adding your VT credentials as a login option. Once you've joined the VT network, you'll be able to join groups specific to Virginia Tech, such as the Housing group. I highly suggest joining the Computer Science group at Virginia Tech as well. There is a lot of information posted there that you can't find elsewhere.
Answer:
Like James said, most classes are held in McBryde Hall, with a few classes in Goodwin, Randolph, Torgersen, and even Buruss Hall. I have a class this semester at the CS department building in KnowledgeWorks II, but generally classes are not held in KWII. In any case, traveling around the campus isn't that hard, and most of the graduate students I know live in off campus housing.

If you are set on living on-campus, the Graduate Life Center provides exclusive housing for graduate students only on campus. It's expensive, but the experience is worth it, IMO. There are also apartments available in the downtown area which can be mostly considered "on-campus".
