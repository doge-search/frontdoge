编辑



Washington State University (WSU) is a public research university based in Pullman, Washington, in the Palouse region of the northwest United States.
Founded in 1890, WSU (colloquially "Wazzu") is the state's land-grant university, well known for its programs in chemical engineering, veterinary medicine, agriculture, pharmacy, neuroscience, food science, plant science, business, architecture, and communications. It is ranked in the top 140 universities in America with high research activity, as determined by the U.S. News & World Report. With an undergraduate enrollment of 24,470 and a total student enrollment of 29,686, it is the second largest institution of higher education in Washington state.
The university also operates campuses across Washington known as WSU Spokane, WSU Tri-Cities, and WSU Vancouver, all founded 27 years ago in 1989. In 1992, WSU launched an Internet-based Global Campus, which includes its online degree program, WSU Online. These campuses award primarily bachelor's and master's degrees. Freshmen and sophomores were first admitted to the Vancouver campus in 2006 and to the Tri-Cities campus in 2007. Total enrollment for the four campuses and WSU Online exceeds 29,686 students. This includes 1,751 international students.
WSU's athletic teams are called the Cougars and the school colors are crimson and gray. The six men's and nine women's varsity teams compete in NCAA Division I in the Pac-12 Conference.

Question:

What are the strongest majors in terms of job prospects and what are the weakest majors at Washington State?
Answer:
I really don't know that they have any "weak" majors at WSU. I know I may be a bit biased (being a Cougar), but assuming the the college has a good program, job prospects are about the graduate...not the college. Where are you willing to move? Who are you not willing to work for? How "needed" is your field, and what "ins" do you have to get there?

I've always thought a history degree was "weak," but there are places that really need people who are interested in doing research and get excited about finding out what came before now, and finding the influences from the past the have made the present - be in a people, a nation, a family, etc.

IMO, if you are looking for a degree that will get you "a job," you are looking at it the wrong way. For the amount of money the you (or someone else) and the time you are going to invest in it, you need to start with "what do I want to be when I grow up?" and mold your education and life choices around that. The answer to that question will lead you, not only to what you will major in, but where you may live, how you will live, how much family time you have, etc.

If you want to be an actor, for instance, you can go to the best acting schools in the country and become a VERY GOOD actor, but if you won't leave Middle of Nowhere, Anystate...well, your job prospects are slim. In that case, I would also suggest that you didn't REALLY want to be a actor.

So figure out what you want to be (not just what you want to do). Study a bit about what that will mean in geography, economics, and security. THEN start looking for a University to help fulfill you dream.

Answer:
I was asked a similar question some years ago, when a young man was trying to weigh in-state colleges in Washington State. Having attended and earned degrees from colleges out of state, I spoke as someone who would potentially hire from the more local colleges.

Washington State is a very strong school in sciences, math, and business. So is the University of Washington. Both schools have good engineering schools, but Washington is stronger in Aeronautical Engineering, where Washington State is excellent at Civil and Electrical Engineering. Washington State would be preferable in terms of mining engineering, too.

WSU is also renowned for their veterinary school, which I understand is very tough to get into. I would project that their biological sciences are strong, too; WSU also has its own zoo on campus.

One thing that impressed me greatly was the emphasis on interdisciplinary studies. For example, there is an actual dairy on campus (with some of the best cheese and ice cream) that is a partnership between animal sciences and dietary sciences programs.

My advice to anyone is to follow your passion, work hard, and be the best. Washington State University is a good school to explore your passion and be able to learn. I have discovered that a degree from WSU reflects the high standards we expect from a top-tier regional school.
