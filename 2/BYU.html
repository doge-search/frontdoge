编辑



Brigham Young University (often referred to as BYU or, colloquially, The Y) is a private research university located in Provo, Utah, United States. It is owned and operated by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS Church), and, excluding online students, is the largest of any religious university and the third largest private university in the United States, with 29,672 on-campus students. Approximately 99 percent of the students are members of the LDS Church, and one-third of its US students are from Utah.
Students attending BYU are required to follow an honor code, which mandates behavior in line with LDS teachings such as academic honesty, adherence to dress and grooming standards, and abstinence from extramarital sex and from the consumption of drugs and alcohol. Many students (88 percent of men, 33 percent of women) either delay enrollment or take a hiatus from their studies to serve as Mormon missionaries. (Men typically serve for two-years, while women serve for 18 months.) An education at BYU is also less expensive than at similar private universities, since "a significant portion" of the cost of operating the university is subsidized by the church's tithing funds.
BYU offers programs in liberal arts, engineering, agriculture, management, physical and mathematical sciences, nursing and law. The university is broadly organized into 11 colleges or schools at its main Provo campus, with certain colleges and divisions defining their own admission standards. The university also administers two satellite campuses, one in Jerusalem and one in Salt Lake City, while its parent organization, the Church Educational System (CES), sponsors sister schools in Hawaii and Idaho. The university's primary focus is on undergraduate education, but it also has 68 master's and 25 doctoral degree programs.
BYU's athletic teams compete in Division I of the NCAA and are collectively known as the BYU Cougars. Their college football team is an NCAA Division I Independent, while their other sports teams compete in either the West Coast Conference or Mountain Pacific Sports Federation. BYU's sports teams have won a total of fourteen national championships.

Question:

Brigham Young University
Answer:
What are the best reasons to attend BYU?
Answer:
I graduated in 2014 with a BS in Biophysics. There are ups and downs to BYU, and I will address the positives.

1. BYU is very rigorous. This prepares you well for a job after graduating. All of my friends that went to class and did their homework have good jobs. Not powerful, high-paying jobs, but they all have jobs where you can make a real living. This also prepares you very well for grad school. I'm getting a grad degree right now and it pales in comparison to the work I had to do for mediocre grades in my undergrad.

2. It's so cheap. Seriously, it's so cheap, and for the quality of education you get, it's a steal. I don't know if there's a better bang-for-your-buck university in all of America.

3. It's pretty easy to get a campus job, and since Provo is inexpensive, you can make enough to cover your living expenses without a problem.

4. If you like the outdoors, Provo is great - close to decent skiing (if you're willing to drive 75 minutes you can get to world-class skiing), rock climbing, there's a lake, hiking, amazing mountains, great roads for road biking (this matters; Colorado might be beautiful but it doesn't have nearly the access to great roads that Utah does), and you can get to SLC in 45 minutes.

If I could do it all over again, I would, except I'd get it done faster.
Answer:
Cost efficiency. The what-you-get-for-what-you-pay ratio is no joke. BYU is a legitimate university with great programs and rock-bottom tuition compared to other private universities.

Minimal nastiness. I have friends who went to other schools and had to deal with the awkwardness of coming home to shameless roommates having sex right there. Answer:
BYU certainly has its own culture, and for people that fit into the culture it's a very peaceful experience. Of course, university studies are stressful to the best of us. But we're studying in a place where other people share our values, most of the students are genuinely considerate and kind, and the most stressful things in life are categorically avoided -- drug problems, hangovers, unplanned pregnancy, and so forth.

For those that don't fit into the culture (they always know who they are), they can choose to have a miserable experience at BYU. The university is exactly what it claims to be: a highly selective conservative Mormon school. The education is great and the value is undeniable, but the strong presence of a niche culture should be assumed.

I'm not saying that everyone at BYU fits a single mold. Take me, for example. I'm very liberal (for a Utah Mormon, anyway), I'm open-minded, and I don't think Obamacare is the worst idea anyone's had since the Holocaust. My politics are not well-liked on campus. But I enjoy being here a lot, and I have an easy enough time finding like-minded friends.

I should admit that I'm not too far from the norm. I am straight, I love and believe in the LDS church in a big way, and I haven't adopted any counterculture fashion trends. I can understand how people on the other end of those spectra would have a difficult time. I like to think they could find a school that fits them.

There is one thing about BYU that seems to me like an incredible advantage, and I'd like to see it studied formally: 99% of the students share a belief in a very complex church with a heavy focus on educating its members in its own history, organization and doctrines -- and this common background is frequently leveraged to explain difficult concepts across hundreds of academic disciplines.

Any tutor knows that the best way to help someone understand something is to relate it to something they already understand. The best tutors can use basketball analogies to teach chemistry, auto mechanics principles to teach math, or video game strategies to teach interior design. BYU professors seem to understand this intuitively, and they're working with entire classrooms that have the LDS church in common -- and with it, thousands of beloved stories and heroes, a respected pioneer legacy, complex doctrines, volumes of ancient scripture, a thriving viral culture, a tendency for self-effacing humor, and so on. Most professors are quick to draw on this wealth of common information for useful comparisons, memory aids, character archetypes, and even jokes. This works really, really well for most of us. It's a catalyst to the whole learning process.

I'd give BYU my highest recommendation. Obviously it's not for everyone. But for those it's meant to serve, there's no better place to study.
