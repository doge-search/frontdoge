Northeastern University

编辑



Northeastern University (NU formerly NEU) is a private research university in Boston, Massachusetts, established in 1898. It is categorized as R1 (Doctoral Universities: Highest Research Activity) by the Carnegie Classification of Institutions of Higher Education.
The university offers undergraduate and graduate programs leading to doctoral degrees on its main campus in the Fenway-Kenmore, Roxbury, South End, and Back Bay neighborhoods, as well as advanced degrees at graduate campuses in Charlotte, North Carolina, Seattle, Washington, and Silicon Valley, California. Also, Northeastern will soon be opening its first international graduate campus in Toronto, Canada. The University has roughly 18,000 undergraduates and nearly 7,000 graduate students.
Northeastern features a cooperative education program that integrates classroom study with professional experience on seven continents and a comprehensive study abroad program. In 2011, the university opened the George J. Kostas Research Institute for Homeland Security. Also, in 2016, the Interdisciplinary Science and Engineering Complex will open.
Northeastern is a large, highly residential university. Most students choose to live on campus but upperclassmen and upperclasswomen may live off campus in many of the city's tree lined residential neighborhoods. The university offers generous financial aid packages. More than 75% of Northeastern students receive some form of financial aid. In 2015-16, the university will offer $239 million in grant and scholarship assistance.
The university's sports teams, the Northeastern Huskies, compete in NCAA Division I as members of the Colonial Athletic Association (CAA) in 18 varsity sports. The men's and women's hockey teams compete in Hockey East, while the men's and women's rowing teams compete in the Eastern Association of Rowing Colleges (EARC) and Eastern Association of Women's Rowing Colleges (EAWRC), respectively. Men's lacrosse competes in the PCLL. In 2013, men's basketball won its first CAA regular season championship, men's soccer won the CAA title for the first time, and women's ice hockey won a record 16th Beanpot championship.

Question:

How is Northeastern University for Masters in Computer Science?
I am speaking in terms of Curriculum, Job opportunities, reputation etc.
Answer:
A2A
I interacted with a professor at Northeastern University when he came for a counselling seminar in Delhi. These are the points he spoke about.
I'm writing what he said from memory. Use it as a guide, if you like, but please verify for yourself before making a decision. Things might have changed since then also.

Pros
They have some really nice programs and tracks for students to choose from.
They have some good research labs.
They have Co-op arrangement with companies for internships.
They have good faculties.
Cons
Their courses are of 4 credits. It makes it difficult for you to transfer credits with any other university.
They have two semesters in a year instead of the usual summer, winter, spring, autumn. So again, it is difficult to transfer credits.
This may also prove to be a problem when applying for PhD at perhaps another university.
Curriculum: The curriculum as I understood in EC and CS are comprehensive and rigorous and taught by some very good and competent professors, if not the best.

Job Opportunities: The said that there are good job opportunities, but listed nice companies where students got recruited. However, I'd take it with a pinch of salt. Job market is ever-changing and dynamic.

Reputation: By his own admission, Northeastern is a new University. Not to be confused with the very prestigious Northwestern. Being a new University it has to stand tall when compared to other Universities of that stature. But, individually, despite being new, it is a very good University and a good place to study in general. It has its own reputation. It has tie-ups with industry and it gets grants, etc.

Hope this helps you out in making a proper decision.

Question:

How is Northeastern University's School of Computer Science and Information as an undergrad?
Answer:
Going to Northeastern for Computer Science is a great idea.

Most undergraduate programs will follow a pretty similar curriculum: intro to programming, data structures, systems, a design class or two, a bit of math, and a few electives to boot. The Northeastern faculty members do a great job of giving students a baseline in all of these areas. Northeastern's standard undergraduate curriculum seems to be a bit less rigorous than some other universities; however, you will have the option to take masters and even doctoral level classes, however, so I believe this is a non-issue.

The co-op program is an opportunity to develop your skills, gain experience, and try new things.

Students take six months, usually two or three times through the course of their education, to go into the world and apply their newfound knowledge. At Northeastern, we call each of those stints a co-op.

My classmates have co-oped at research labs at the best universities in the country, defense contractors in DC, mega-corps like Google and Facebook, and start ups that are doing cool and impactful stuff. Some even started their own companies. That experiential learning is a cornerstone of Northeastern’s culture all but guarantees that you not only learn stuff but you learn how the stuff you learned can be useful too. You would be hard-pressed to find someone at Northeastern who has not had an interesting and impactful professional experience by the time they graduate.

Co-op is also an opportunity to try new things. Are you considering graduate school? Work at as a researcher at a lab. Have you always wanted to live in California? Get a co-op there and see how you like it. What about Thailand or Ireland? There are hundreds of positions outside the United States.

The college, university, and Boston provide great communities for technology and beyond.

The college is small enough at about 140 students per year that most people know each other on a first name basis. There is a weekly event on campus for makers to meet and collaborate. Clubs like ACM, Scout, WiT regularly host speakers — students, faculty, entrepreneurs, and big-company representatives who want to share their knowledge with students.

Beyond the college, Northeastern provides numerous resources for students to become engaged with their communities, develop their interests, and experience the world through study abroad programs. I spent my first two summers in China and London, for example.

And, if that isn’t enough, the campus is located in the heart of Boston, which has an incredibly large and diverse community and numerous events that are open to college students. Over my few years here, I attended McHacks, HackMIT x2, HackBeanpot, Boston I/O, and met Peter Thiel at MIT. And, I have barely scratched the surface of what the community can offer.

And we are getting even better.

Since 2006, we have gone up over fifty spots in USNWR. Admission to the college, especially CCIS, is becoming more selective. Top companies are increasing their recruiting effort. Administrators are mindful of the curriculum and looking for ways to improve. When I recently spoke with Dean Brodley, she outlined the courses that need revamping and spoke about the measures the college is taking to make sure undergraduates come out well-prepared. Some courses, like Prof. Wilson's Networks and Distributed Systems are really top notch and ensure students walk away with a practical and functional understanding of the subject matter.

tl;dr: Going to Northeastern for Computer Science is a great idea.
Answer:
For me it has been an amazing experience. I came into Northeastern in the Fall of 2012 with no programming experience, and was really intimidated. After a few semesters I felt confident in my ability to contribute as a active member of a company on coop.

The coop program is amazing. There are so many companies looking for students to work for them. The companies range from large companies (Microsoft, Google, Intuit, Amazon, Square, Yelp, Apple) to start ups. Most students go on 2-3 coops, which allows you to experience different sized companies and see different cultures. This is amazing for you to figure out what you want in a company. The CS coop advisors are an amazing resource for professional development.

I have been lucky to work for Microsoft for my first coop, and I am starting my second coop (Facebook) on Monday.

The classes have been good/ok. I came in with no experience, so the first year of classes was amazing. Since then I have had a few higher level CS courses, and only found one or two that were challenging and interesting. I have not been able to explore too many elective classes yet, so take my perspective with a grain of salt. I do feel that this is changing with the new Dean we have hired, who has a specialty in Machine Learning. They are hiring a bunch of new faculty to teach some really cool stuff.

Outside of classes, there are a bunch of professors doing really interesting research. Professors love talking about their research, and there are plenty of opportunities as a student to get involved in research projects.

The other students are so smart and awesome. It really does feel like a community. There are some really cool student groups and clubs for CS too.

I definitively did not cover it all, so feel free to follow up.
