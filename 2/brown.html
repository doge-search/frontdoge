Brown University

编辑



Brown University is a private, Ivy League research university in Providence, Rhode Island. Founded in 1764 as "The College in the English Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations," Brown is the seventh-oldest institution of higher education in the United States and one of the nine Colonial Colleges established before the American Revolution.
At its foundation, Brown was the first college in the United States to accept students regardless of their religious affiliation. Its engineering program, established in 1847, was the first in the Ivy League. Brown's New Curriculum sometimes referred to in education theory as the Brown Curriculum was adopted by faculty vote in 1969 after a period of student lobbying; the New Curriculum eliminated mandatory "general education" distribution requirements, made students "the architects of their own syllabus," and allowed them to take any course for a grade of satisfactory or unrecorded no-credit. In 1971, Brown's coordinate women's institution, Pembroke College, was fully merged into the university. Pembroke Campus now operates as a place for dorms and classrooms.
Undergraduate admissions is selective, with an acceptance rate of 9 percent for the class of 2020, according to the university. The University comprises The College, the Graduate School, Alpert Medical School, the School of Engineering, the School of Public Health, and the School of Professional Studies (which includes the IE Brown Executive MBA program). Brown's international programs are organized through the Watson Institute for International and Public Affairs, and the university is academically affiliated with the Marine Biological Laboratory and the Rhode Island School of Design. The Brown/RISD Dual Degree Program, offered in conjunction with the Rhode Island School of Design, is a five-year course that awards degrees from both institutions.
Brown's main campus is located in the College Hill Historic District in the city of Providence, the third largest city in New England. The University's neighborhood is a federally listed architectural district with a dense concentration of Colonial era buildings. On the western edge of the campus, Benefit Street contains "one of the finest cohesive collections of restored seventeenth- and eighteenth-century architecture in the United States".
Brown has produced 7 Nobel Prize winners, 57 Rhodes Scholars, five National Humanities Medalists, eight billionaire graduates, and 10 National Medal of Science laureates, and has also produced Fulbright, Marshall, and Mitchell scholars.

1，学在布朗
布朗大学位于美国罗德岛州普罗维登斯市，建校于1764年。校园在普罗维登斯市东边的一座号称“学院山”的小土坡上，校园里的建筑大都是古朴优雅的维多利亚时代风格的建筑。学校主要由本科生院，研究生院，医学院，工程学院，公共卫生学院组成。
布朗大学最出名的地方在于其自由的本科生”开放教纲“。布朗大学的所有课程都可以由学生自主选择，本科生入学后在前两年并没有必修课，只需根据兴趣自由发展，然后在第二年末确定专业方向。想取得布朗大学的学士学位，你只需要满足四点：一，修满至少三十个学分；二，确定专业方向完成专业必修课；三，证明自己的写作能力；四，完成八学期注册。（另外挂科不会出现在成绩单上。）

Brown在本科阶段的教育也许最突出的特点就是Open Curriculum了，在
CUUS
上读到过一篇谈对于Open Curriculum感想的帖子，内容如下：
近两年Brown University似乎在本申圈子里火爆程度有所提高。不知道是由于top tier college education还是拜Emma Watson所赐。总之，由一个五年前在国内几乎没有什么知名度的学校变成了申请大热。昨天在校内看了个贴说去年大陆近700人申请Brown，录了20来个。具体数据怎么样不清楚，但是基本上八九不离十吧。
话说回来，几乎所有人对Brown的考虑都是带有双重逻辑的，尤其是那些在高中最outstanding最aggressive的孩子们。一方面，ivy的成员，不错的open curriculum选项，瞎玩还能不挂科的pass/fail grading system，LAC级别的师生比例。这些都是作为一所独特的top级别学校拥以立身的资本。不过另一方面，虽然有光环，但似乎总是没有HYP CU Wharton来得耀眼。虽然课程设置丰富自由，但就是没有商学院工程学院这些一毕业就almost 6 figure annual salary guarantee的设置。虽然教授非常available and helpful，但就是没有Nobel laureate或者总统顾问坐镇。总的来说，这些不错但并非顶尖的条件make Brown an option，但不是best choice。在Brown读书不够exciting，不够thrilling，光环不够耀眼，前程不够远大。Brown比较适合文学青年和自由主义者。至于有着远大志向的同学们，基本上可以出门右转了。
以上这些完全出自作者本人，一个热爱学校热爱学术的好学生。应该可以作为一个公正的student evaluation。Brown管理层聊不了解这些呢？很显然他们完全了解、泰然处之并且还没有任何改变的意图。山下的RISD依然是RISD，没有变成Brown University school of art。前几年的时候，某校友提出弄些钱来整个商学院，校方直接婉拒回绝掉了，理由是什么不清楚，大概是不想“带坏小孩子”。Brown一直坐在lower ivy的位置上悠然自得，别的事情基本不管。你看这学校都不急，做学生的自然也就放任自流了。
总的来说呢，当你深入了解Brown之后，就会发现这里完全是一个专心致志搞学习的去处，是一个宅的地方。这里的强项不是学术，便是文艺。使得它的气氛很微妙：严肃而又散漫。一切都发自内心，发自每一个独特的个体，让本来就小的校园愈发感觉零散。在Brown完全无法感受到抱团或者扎堆：大家都各干各的事情，鲜少有exciting enough的事情能聚集一大堆人的目光。在Brown，学习和生活是完全出自于个人兴趣的。基本上每个人都宅在各自的地方，干着各自的事情，时不时会小范围讨论一下。本校的学生宅起来学习。外校转来的学生也忽然发现，他们唯一的选择也是宅起来学习。学习和宅是这里的主流。这种学术是纯粹的、完全出自兴趣的。Brown的学生不怎么关注未来，不怎么关注结果。他们只是在单纯地享受这些过程。Why nothing special? 因为客观条件的不允许造就了这种气氛。Providence就是一个让无聊的人更加感到无聊的地方。
所以除了学习上网和睡觉之外，还可以做什么？这是适合来Brown与否的核心问题。严格来说很多人去其它的学校，都会拥有比在Brown读书更好的前途。并非他们不够优秀，而是Brown并不关注你的优秀和出众。有人说Brown是最难出现“校园之星”的地方，因为这里的价值观过于松散，以至于很难找到大环境上的一致认同和迁就。在一些更加professional oriented的学校，校方组织的机会，比如说job panel，social activity会非常多。这些活动对于那些原意stand out and perform的学生而言机不可失。他们被这些主动提供的机会“压迫”着参与其中，从而获得不断地充实感。Brown类似的机会并无太多。找不到自己愿意做的事情，就会闲着，然后浪费时间，渐渐地失去目标。
开放课程和pass/fail本身的意义，其实远远超过了字面能够理解到的。一个学生在Brown完全可以自由地追求自己的兴趣，而丝毫不妨碍他今后的工作发展和深造。符合兴趣的好玩课程可以和枯燥刻板的“专业课”广泛并存在成绩单上，得到A也并非难事。这里的学习压力和其它top比起来很小，多余的时间都可以用来做自己喜欢的事情。从而留给学生们很大的空间。这里的教授也会利用他们相当之多的空闲时间帮助学生，而不是沉浸于自己的研究之中。况且，他们手上的资源相当之多。唯独只是需要你的主动性。
我个人在Brown的经历和当初的期望差别非常大，这都是因为学校的open所给予的空间。在这里学生可以有更多的时间探索个人的天赋和思考自己究竟想要什么。而不是为一大堆core疲于奔命或者拘束于过往的期望。对于普通学生而言，强制性的综合课程的确可以让他更加全面发展。但是如果想在一个领域做到优秀出众，专注和适当的沉静是不可或缺的。Brown是一个沉默的学校，它鼓励学生以提高自己的内在，也就是作为“人”的内禀素质为目标，而不是急于给他们传授在社会上生存的技能。当然，实现这些完全都靠自己，在任何一所学校都可以做到这样。Brown只是提供了更好的条件。
所以对于毕业之后急于找工作投身wall street或者industry的同学们来说，Brown的确并非一个好的选择。但是对于想利用本科四年好好学习打基础的人，这里是一个ideal choice。没有太多闲杂事务的烦扰，没有多余诱惑的考验。全身心地投入到学习和思考之中，四年之后你会发现自己能得到未曾预料的成果。

Question:

What does Brown University look for in prospective CS master's students?
Answer:
Our master's program is designed for a broad range of students:

Some have extensive prior computing experience (say equivalent to our own bachelor's degrees, or alternatively lots of work experience), are are interested in going quite deep into computer science but not at the level of a PhD. Either they want to study one area in depth (like a very, very “lite” PhD), or they want to broaden their horizons all across CS.
Some have more limited prior computing—say a major from a smaller liberal arts college—and have had not had much exposure to the latest in computer science. This is their chance to catch up to researchy computer science.
Some have no prior formal computing at all, but have prior experience that makes them ready to take courses in the subject. For them, this is their introduction to formal computing.

Therefore, there is not one uniform set of expectations of prospective students.

However, there is one thing we are absolutely concerned about. Our courses are very tough (sometimes startlingly so to students from other institutions). The other students in the room are our undergrads and PhD students, who are absolutely top-caliber students. Therefore, it's important that our master's students be able to hold their own against this competition. Otherwise, either they would be left behind or the whole class would have to lower its standards. Therefore, we are looking for evidence that students can handle the rigor of our program.

What we are not looking for is “well-rounded, interesting” students, the way undergrad admissions appears to. However, interesting backgrounds that are relevant to the course of study—e.g., industrial experience, experience running a large ISP, experience doing real-world networking, as just a few examples—are all certainly useful, though by themselves certainly insufficient to be admitted.

Hope this helps. Happy to answer additional questions.

Is it necessary to get a master's degree in computer science in order to advance in the tech industry?
Answer:
Most of the answers here are really bad, because they don't point out the weakness in the question. It's all a function of what the student's prior preparation is. If you have a strong bachelor's in computer science from a top CS school (Stanford, CMU, Brown, …), the master's will hardly matter except in very specialized instances. But as you start peeling away those assumptions, the master's becomes more and more relevant:
It's not a strong bachelor's.
It's not from a strong program.
It's not even in computer science.
There are additional factors, such as using a master's to gain entry into another country where you would like to end up working. In this case, it may be near essential.

Note that I'm not a full-fledged advocate of CS master's degrees. I actually discourage many of my own students from them [*]. But the question is too broad, and there are many ways it can be interpreted, and for some interpretations the answer is a definitive yes and for others a definitive no.

[*] I do this in cases where they already have strong bachelor's from Brown, in which case they are more qualified, I would say, than most master's students from many other places. In particular, I suggest that they wait a few years before getting a master's so as to become current with the state of computer science then, rather than doing it now, when they are already fairly current. For instance, a student who graduated ten years ago probably had little to no coursework on distributed systems (we always had courses, but not that many students realized their importance), whereas you can't escape it now. Similarly for many aspects of machine learning, software development (apps), programming languages, etc. In the same way, who knows what ten more years will bring.

For advancement in a technical company, you first of all need to perform. After the initial hire, you are evaluated on what you accomplish, whether you can be depended on, whether you come through, and things like that. As you work for a company you will quickly learn a lot about what you don't know. Some of that might be easily learned on the job or in a company's own training program. For example, I had to learn how radar systems work in order to move ahead at a particular point in my career, and at a later point in my career I taught internal corporate courses on managing software projects to people with CS degrees who had no clue about how to properly estimate the cost of a project or track its progress. But not all can be learned from internal training courses. A masters degree can fill in gaps in your knowledge that may not become apparent until you have worked for a few years. For example, a lot of people get masters degrees in software engineering because they realize that most CS degrees don't really prepare them for true software engineering jobs. But you have to get a genuine software engineering program (too many schools offer computer science and call it software engineering). A big advantage you have if you work for a "hi tech" company is that most of them will help you pay the cost of a masters degree and some will help you select one that is a good fit to what the company needs.

I always recommend examining any proposed masters degree from the perspective of a hi-tech employer. Does the degree cover things that the employer wants you to know more about? If not, the degree might be a waste of time from the job advancement perspective. Few employers will automatically give you a raise or promotion just for getting a masters degree - although some educational and government organizations will. On the other hand, you may find yourself more likely to be selected for certain job assignments based on your academic degrees. And interesting job assignments are sometimes "opportunities to excel". In other words, they are tough assignments and they give you a chance to show your value to the company, which leads to promotions and raises and such.
