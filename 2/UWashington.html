The University of Washington, commonly referred to as simply Washington, UW, or informally U-Dub, is a public flagship research university based in Seattle, Washington, United States. Founded in 1861, Washington is one of the oldest universities on the West Coast and features one of the most highly regarded medical schools in the world.
The university has three campuses: the primary and largest in the University District of Seattle and two others in Tacoma and Bothell. Its operating expenses and research budget for fiscal year 2014-15 is expected to be $6.4 billion. The UW occupies over 500 buildings, with over 20 million gross square footage of space, including the University of Washington Plaza, consisting of the 325-foot (99 m) UW Tower and conference center.
Washington is a member of the Association of American Universities. Its research budget is among the highest in the United States. In athletics, the university competes in the NCAA Division I Pac-12 Conference (Pac-12).

讲体验就不得不提学校所在的城市。我最初听到西雅图的时候内心里只有三个关键词——“下雨”、“大城市”和《北京遇上西雅图》。虽然后来才知道《北京遇上西雅图》并没有在西雅图取景，但这部电影着实向广大中国人民普(wu)及(dao)了这个城市。
地理
——西雅图，位于美国的西北角，是华盛顿州最大的城市，离加拿大温哥华仅两个多小时的车程。
气候
——虽然西雅图纬度与哈尔滨相近，但气候要温和得多。春秋两季气温长期维持在10度到20度之间；冬季稍冷，最冷时0度左右，不过室内都有供暖很暖和，每年差不多都能见到一次雪【至于有一个回答中说的一下雪就停课，反正我是没有享受过】；夏天我没有经历过，但听说有时会因为没有空调而酷暑难耐。作为一个深圳来的宝宝，我第一次到西雅图时从下飞机开始就觉得冷得不行，然而现在似乎是适应了这种微凉，经常穿着只穿两件就在外面晃荡...
下雨
——传闻中一年下雨300天的说法不无道理，但实际体验上只能说总体雨天略多于晴天。西雅图的雨季在秋冬季，一般从学校秋季开学起就会比较频繁的下雨。但很少出现瓢泼大雨，大多数时候只是毛毛细雨，路上的学生大多穿着防水的外套很少有人打伞，所以我也渐渐养成了不打伞的习惯...下雨的时候也大多只是早晚下雨，一整天连绵不断的雨也不常出现。
城市规模
——若以大西雅图地区而论，总人口约四百万人，是美国第15大都会区；但如只以西雅图这个城市本身而言的话，人口在六十万人左右，在美国也只勉强算是个大城市了。比较繁华的地方有Downtown、Chinatown(International District)和学校所在的University District。而我个人的体验是，如果不往附近稍远一些的地方（如Mt. Rainier或Leavenworth）跑的话，西雅图的新鲜感大概只能维持一个月。
2. 学校篇
华盛顿大学，简称华大或UW（发音为UDub），2016年US News全美排名52，世界排名11【不要问我这是什么鬼逻辑...我也不知道】。
校园
——华大有Seattle，Bothell和Tacoma三个独立的校区。主校区也就是Seattle校区位于西雅图的University District。学校旁边就是华盛顿湖，天气好的时候站在学校中心的Red Square上就能眺望到远处的瑞尼尔雪山，整体风景还是很不错的。华大校园不算大，如果按最远的对角线走的话大概需要二十分钟。学校的建筑一半是古老的哥特式风格一半是近年修建的现代建筑，但两者之间结合得很好。春天樱花盛开的时候在校园内漫步能够有效的缓解冬季阴雨可能带来的坏心情。
交通
——都说在美国没有车是寸步难行的，但是在西雅图好像倒不是很成立。学校周围的公交站停靠的公交线路几乎覆盖了西雅图的各个方向，今年三月连接机场的轻轨也马上就要通到学校了。而学生使用校卡就能免费乘坐公交车和机场轻轨。所以在交通方面我给99分，剩下1分我怕西雅图骄傲。
饮食
——学校周边吃的分三大块，一块是学校食堂，另一块是University Village的稍高端的餐厅，最后一块是University Way上比较日常的饮食。食堂的食物种类就比较固定，永远是披萨汉堡沙拉意面这几大类，偶尔会有各国的特色菜式。另外两块都有各国的餐厅可供选择，中国菜也很多，但是住了几年之后也就觉得没有什么好吃的了。学校周围也有很多大型超市，平时有时间自己做菜的话会比较复合自己口味，也会比较省钱。饮食这一方面就给个及格分60吧。
住宿
——华大有自己的学生宿舍，北边是旧宿舍，房间比较小也比较老；西边的新宿舍比较新但也贵。然而住学校最大的问题是大多数宿舍要求强制购买每年清空的dinning plan，这个plan只能在学校食堂使用，而学校食堂......当然也可以选择住在校外，学校周边布满了给学生居住的公寓或者house，价格区间分布的也很广，跟别的美国城市比价格不算高，耐心总能找到适合自己的。我第一年住在宿舍，第二年住校外，第三年预计还会住在校外。总体80分吧。
教学
——华大实行的是quarter制，一年四个quarter，连续上三个就能选择休息一个。一般是默认summer quarter休息，所以暑假就是从六月中旬放到九月底。但也可以选择winter quarter休息回家过年。每个quarter的时间大概都是10周加上1周考试周，在不多交学费的基础上我们一般可以选3到4门课，而大多数课程在10周的时间里就要上完一整本书的内容，还有至少三次的exam。我个人觉得在学术上的压力还是不低的。
专业
——我不知道别的学校是怎么样，但是华大大部分学生在进校时是没有专业的，而是需要在满足该专业的prerequisite之后再进行申请。而其中很多competitive的专业如CSE, Biology, Business的竞争都是不小的，对GPA会有比较高的要求。当然也有那些竞争不是很激烈的专业就比较容易进。但在这专业这一点总体上我是很不满的【才不是因为当初以为自己被录的时候进了商科结果实际上过了一年半还是没进呢哼】
其他的我想到了再补充吧。

Question:

What is it like to be a computer science major at the University of Washington?
Answer:
Introduction

I'm going to talk about the Undergraduate experience at the UW Computer Science Department (UWCSE), having graduated from the program in 2013. Short answer - it's pretty darn awesome.

The long answer lies below.

** - Indicates that I talk more about this in a later section.

Beginnings, Entry

(The Paul Allen Center for Computer Science and Engineering)

There are a few different ways (most) students become part of the UW Computer Science and Engineering Department (UWCSE). The important ones are Direct Admits and General Admissions.

Before I get into what those mean, know that the UW has two introductory courses in Computer Science that are open for the entire university to take - CSE 142 and CSE 143. These cover Computer Science fundamentals ranging from basic loops to complex data structures using the Java language.

That being said, let's get to it. Admission to UWCSE is separate from UWashington university admissions. The Department offers two undergraduate degrees - a Bachelor's in Computer Science (CS) and a Bachelor's in Computer Engineering (CE). The first, offered through the school of Arts and Sciences is more flexible and geared to let you choose whereas the latter, through the School of Engineering is more rigid and more hardware inclusive in its requirements. The core content is the same in both, just the method of delivery being different.

Here's former (and popular) lecturer Marty Stepp rapping for an introductory class. He spits out his own rhymes and top submissions from the class.


The Direct Admits (DAs) to the department are usually in-state (definitely US based) students who seem like outstanding prospects coming out of high school. They form a small percentage of the department's body.

The General Admits form the bulk of the Department. They are the people who do reasonably well in 142 and 143 (especially 143) (EDIT - or some equivalent) and other Department prerequisites (linked below for specificity) of math, science, writing and apply when they are eligible.

Entry into the department is highly competitive - (EDIT - corrected by advising) acceptance rates have been in the 30 to 40% range over the last few years but there is a very helpful advising staff (links below) to guide and mold you through the process.

The First Year in the Department
Ok, you're in! Congratulations! Admission into the department means roughly a few things:

1. You are now eligible to start taking majors only Computer Science courses with other UWCSE students. Assuming you're past 142, 143 (EDIT - or whatever equivalent) you start taking compulsory 300-level courses that will form the core structure of your CS education. There are usually 5-7 of these dealing with theory, hardware and software and 2 CS courses a quarter is considered a good amount of work. 3 can be painful but doable for some. 4 (EDIT, earlier said definitely) has been known get you an email from a concerned advisor questioning your decision making skills. But some do dare. There is no shame in taking 1 a quarter a few times either.


(The Main Atrium + Cafe)

2. You get access to the super beautiful Paul Allen Center and the Undergraduate Labs. For many UWCSE-ers these two places turn into second (first?) homes over their undergrad lives.


(An Undergrad lab at UWCSE)


The labs have PC and Linux Computers (and laptop zones) preloaded with most packages needed for courses and also form a great place to meet people, get help with HW and projects or just chill.

3. A lot of very important UWCSE events become accessible to you. These include the Career Fair, the Research Fair, student organized festivals and visits from various companies in the area.**

4. You start to realize that everyone around you is super smart and motivated. Standing out from the pack will become harder for most. But don't worry, the Department is aware of this and will take care of you.

5. You start seeing that CS focused companies bring a lot of free food.

The next few years
After emerging tempered by the 300s and the new sights, you now have a wealth of 400-level courses to take according to your interests. Artificial Intelligence, Computer Security, Operating Systems, Computer Architecture are just some of the cool things you can learn. Most undergrads end up taking 4 to 8 of these over 1 to 2 years depending on when they start. Again the Direct Admits have a lot more time to choose. Most students also do a Capstone Project to top off their undergrad careers, where they take on a real world, hard problem under a capstone banner (Games, Developing World, Hardware, Web Services...) and work in teams to kick some... well... you know. Also by this time, many students have internships at CS companies** and/or have done research** with UWCSE faculty**. After a grueling few years, you are done :)

Post UWCSE
UWCSE undergrads are very employable in industry (Microsoft, Amazon and Google are the biggest individual hirers) and many of us also go to top research schools for Graduate degrees in CS, Mathematics and related fields. It's a good degree to have.


(Working away in a hardware lab)

Now, getting to a few environmental details.

Peers - Social, The Student ACM Chapter
There is a student body affiliated with the ACM who take charge of organizing student events like fairs, company visits and social outings like Barbecues and department sponsored student fests. They also have chill events like movie nights, canoeing and other mixers.Their officers are elected every year - ranging from President to Community Relations to many other friendly and useful posts. The officers are good people to ask about what's going on and everyone motivated is encouraged to become one.


(The Annual CSE BBQ hosted by the Students ACM. Good food, beer *conditions apply* and good people. Credit Prof. Hemingway, linked below)

Peers - Demographics
The Department (like most other engineering ones) has a lot of guys but actually does have a higher female population than most other similar institutions. They don't let anyone in who doesn't meet a certain bar for performance but do search harder for and promote interest in girls in the University and around Seattle. Again like most engineering schools there is also strong representation from Asian/Indian and International backgrounds. The Department values all sorts of diversity and everyone I've met has been open and welcoming. I've also noticed that the quirk factor is strong amongst UWCSE-ers with a high percentage of kids having some sort of art/geek/tech hobby or obsession on the side. You will make friends if you decide to start meeting people.


(A friendly CS Major, not me, in the labs. They may be fed but beware of them following you around with a dazed, hungry look.)

Peers - Competition
As I said earlier, the admission rates into the Department are sadly low due to a variety of reasons (competition, funding...). This means that if you're in, you are one amongst several very bright kids mostly used being at the upper end of most academic things they've done. Again, for most kids this will end here and many feel average despite putting in long hours and maintaining a level of heightened performance. There will be some people who make you feel very dumb and slow, you will learn to get over it, boost your strengths and erase your weaknesses.

The Department knows of this, desires this to some degree and will make sure that the course averages are high in general. An average grade in a UWCSE course usually translates to somewhere in the 3.3 to 3.5 range, much higher than other University courses. This being said, 99% of your peers are helpful and friendly and the competition you feel is primarily against yourself. The fact that everyone is working so hard usually leads to a sense of camaraderie and togetherness manifests itself in social gestures, bemused memes and late nights spent together in labs, apartments or online banging heads over coursework. This melting pot of sharp minds will mean that you have very, very rich resources to learn from and collaborate with both now and in the future.


(An evening on the UWCSE balcony, 6th floor).

UWCSE is consistently ranked amongst the top 10 CS schools in the United States and I happen to think that over the last decade it has a solid claim to be top 5. Especially given Seattle's unique position as a powerhouse in the technology, software and biotech worlds there is a lot of money and opportunity aimed at us. If you put in the work you will hold your own against peers from MIT, Stanford, Carnegie Melon and other top schools and you will never have to second guess your degree.

People - Faculty
Part of the UW being a top CS destination means that we do end up having very highly regarded faculty. This includes downright leaders in several CS disciplines and the best part is that they are all very available. Everyone has office hours, open doors and most importantly, most Professors personally teach courses instead of having a grad student do so. This means that you are directly next to some of the brightest engineering (and overall) minds and they are all very willing to interact and collaborate with you. Also, the UW constantly adds to this pool by having some of the strongest recruiting classes of new Professors (almost always from very elite universities) every year. As an undergrad, they are a goldmine of knowledge, advise and opportunity.

People - Admin
The Department supports its students wholeheartedly with a large and engaged advising staff who guide you through everything from admissions, course choices, future employment, stress, I-need-someone-to-talk-to-now-moments-and everything in the middle. Internal emails from them will keep you informed and connected. Also, there is a regular event called Lunch with the Chair where the Department orders lunch for students to sit down with the Chair+Advising staff and ask anything they want about Departmental and CS life. It's a great way to know and influence the decision making process.

Opportunities - Industry
The Department has one of the best Industrial relations teams I've ever seen. The Department has a high rate of employment with CS being in high demand. Seattle is home to the HQs of giants like Microsoft, Amazon and Boeing and many medical companies and research institutions. It has significant offices of Google, Facebook, Salesforce, a large number of startups. The Department has close ties with practically everyone in the area and across the United States and makes sure they visit the Department frequently.




The UWCSE Career Fair happens twice a year (Fall and Winter Quarters) and is second to none in connecting you with internship and full-time job opportunities from eager to-be employers. Most students manage to snag an internship after Junior year and an increasing number after Sophomore and Freshman years. The common advise is to do as many as you can.They are great sources of real world experience, meeting new people from across the country and of course, making some good money. Microsoft, Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon and Twitter are some common destinations for interns and you will spot a lot of their swag in T-shirts, stickers and posters all through the undergrad realms.


(Career Fair pictures from Prof. Hemingway - linked below)

Interview help, resume revisions, introductions and new opportunities are all plentiful through essentially everyone around. Several companies also visit the Department to connect directly with students through workshops, technical talks, hackathons and other events. The free food is strong with them. Recently they started up a Startup only Fair that was packed with new startups from across the US all hungry for UWCSE talent. Whether going for a fulltime, alumnus or internship, you will be encouraged to polish that resume and step up to the plate.


(The man himself at a talk in the Atrium. The Department has a sizeable Microsoft influence - the _Paul Allen Center_, the _Microsoft_ Atrium, several sponsored labs and so on. We get several influential and brilliant speakers a year who give public talks in this space. Credit Prof. Hemingway, linked below).

Opportunities - Research
The highly engaged faculty that we have works on cutting edge problems across all major CS disciplines, whether they are theoretical, applied, hardware or software in nature. All undergrads are encouraged to take part in Ugrad-Research. The way it works is that you talk to a potential host in a Professor or Graduate student whose work interests you. If there is a match you start helping them on their problems. If you are motivated, they will help you with your own research interests. There is a biannual Research Fair where all research labs looking for students/to get their word out with put up posters and connect with the Undergrads. Mind you, this is serious, cutting edge work and a very rare opportunity to do the real stuff. If you want to go to grad school for CS, this is a very obvious step to take. Most undergrad researchers present at least in the Departmental and University research symposiums. Some get to go to conferences around the world to present their research.

Opportunities - Teaching
Many undergrads serve as teaching assistants for courses that they've taken+done well in. This includes holding office hours, grading and answering questions of desperate students. If you get the chance to TA the introductory 142, 143 courses, you also get to run your own weekly section of new and impressionable kids mystified by the world of Computer Science. Ooooh.

Miscellaneous scenery
Free food, industry swag, loving and living the labs/Paul Allen Center, intense debates on solving HW problems (but independently understanding the solution), an amazing balcony on the 6th floor with a great view of lake Washington. Free printing courtesy Microsoft - big boon to undergrads.

Conclusions
Obviously I can't cover it all. As my answer reflects, I really enjoyed my UWCSE stay and graduated in Computer Science this year. I'd be interested in hearing from someone who didn't have a good experience studying here. My language in this answer is probably familiar to most UWCSE students and graduates making a case for the Department. I'm happy to answer any questions (as will my fellow UWCSE-er Quorans)/make edits regarding this. I hope there was enough specific information in this answer.

It's true there is no literal Master's degree in "user experience" per se (since it's a faux term that gained popularity via the dot-com boom of the 90s), but there are several very good graduate programs in Interaction Design and Human-Computer Interaction, including my own school Carnegie Mellon University (both the IxD program via the School of Design and HCI via the School of Computer Science), Univ of Michigan, Berkeley's i-School, and others at SVA, NYU, and SCAD.

It's unfortunate the previous answer says that "all are utter crap", which says more about that person's outlook than the programs themselves. Yes, industry moves fast, but there needs to be a place for "slow learning" of principles, values, and theories that enable a richer, nuanced, informed perspective on how to design (methods) and more importantly, why we design--purposes and aims. Want to be more than a wireframer or mock-up artist, sensibly challenging execs and clients with clear, tactful, strong rationale? Having such background knowledge isn't just "pie in the sky" theory--it will definitely help assert your place at "the table", shaping and directing the nature of design projects. I use such learnings all the time (and I graduated from CMU in 2001, over a decade ago!) when challenging fellow designers and leadership teams. You will come across as credible and astute, not just some "trendy designer" persisting inaccurate notions of "UX" and "design".

Such programs, of varying postures (cog psych, rhetoric, business, fine arts), offer frameworks, conceptual toolkits, and valuable studio-based learning experiences to explore novel ideas at a more intense, deep level than "Mach 3 industry". This enriches your sense of self as a designer, elevates your skills and goals, and also shapes an outlook on where you see yourself in industry as a well-rounded design professional who can contribute to (and help expand) the field in a valuable manner: speaking, publishing, teaching, mentoring, strategizing, etc. Graduate design programs help you do that and more.

In selecting "the best program" you need to assess for yourself what it is that really interests you about "user experience" and "design", and what do you want to go deeply into, to gain that rich foundation that you can take into industry and push the field further.

And...yes, actually you can get more than just an entry-level job with an MDes or an MFA in Interaction Design / HCI. We just hired a couple folks at mid-senior level for our team straight out of the Master's program at CMU. It depends on your past experience, passion, creative thinking, tactical production, and willingness to learn and grow, regardless of program. Good luck!

My two cents, from someone who holds a bachelor in media technology and has studied cognitive science and UX for two years (working on turning those credits into a degree).

Those studies and my later career (co-founding a high growth digital agency) and the hard-earned experience that brought me has strengthened my view that UX has little to do with technology. You can be a master JavaScript dev but have no clue about how the human psyche is wired or what makes a design intuitive or obvious in its use. UX finds its roots in human endeavors that predate semiconductors by millennia.

But in today's Internet startup world, the terms really tend to overlap. People asking for a UX'er think they want a programmer who makes stuff that works. In reality though, I think it's hard to be really good at both of those. There are those that are but they have generally gone from one to the other. Not aimed at getting great at both, at once. My recommendation to you is that you specialize in either, nurture an interest in the other, but don't go after both at once.

In my opinion, UX requires a certain perspective or mindset, a set of values of what's important. Standing up for the "idiot" user, admire the inconsistencies and limitations of human psychology and being a humanist who works with and not against our biological shortcomings. Understanding and gaining those values takes practice and studying.

Development of software relies on other values of practice and they're not always compatible with the former. UX designers don't come up with great designs through logical analysis. There's room for spontaneity and lateral thinking. Why it's wise to take UX out of the context of libraries, frameworks, widgets and preprogrammed controls and design on a bigger canvas. The iPhone was not born in an IDE.

The courses I look back at now as being most useful to me didn't concern programming but the scientific method and being able to critically analyze your own thought processes. Learning to interview people and write field notes as a way to observe what they did, rather than what they said, form the foundation in how I strive to design for what people need. Not what they say they need ("faster horses").

So I recommend you choose what school your heart tells you that you belong to. Start from there. Then find the bridges to the other side and don't let conventional thinking in either camp hold you back in regards to what you choose to do.
