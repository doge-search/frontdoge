编辑



The University of Kentucky (UK) is a public co-educational university in Lexington, Kentucky. Founded in 1865 by John Bryan Bowman as the Agricultural and Mechanical College of Kentucky, the university is one of the state's two land-grant universities (the other being Kentucky State University), the largest college or university in the state, with 29,385 students as of Fall 2013, and the highest ranked research university in the state according to U.S. News and World Report.
The institution comprises 16 colleges, a graduate school, 93 undergraduate programs, 99 master programs, 66 doctoral programs, and four professional programs. The University of Kentucky has fifteen libraries on campus. The largest is William T. Young Library, a federal depository, hosting subjects related to social sciences, humanities, and life sciences collections. In recent years, the university has focused expenditures increasingly on research, following a compact formed by the Kentucky General Assembly in 1997. The directive mandated that the university become a Top 20 public research institution, in terms of an overall ranking to be determined by the university itself, by the year 2020.

Question:

What are the best reasons to attend the University of Kentucky?
Answer:
This question is really, really important to me as attending the University of Kentucky was a defining moment in my life. Full disclaimer: my experiences at UK and post are not remotely representative of the general UK undergraduate experience, and my post-UK experiences are really, really not representative of life after UK. YMMV.

I will start with the best reasons to attend UK and then tell the other side of the story of the worst reasons to attend UK.

Best reasons to attend UK:

You want the liberating freedom of a debt-free bachelor's education. One of the best ways to have control over your future is to have 1) an education that empowers you to seize opportunities and 2) the financial freedom to make good choices over the longterm. Student debt, particularly silly levels of student debt taken on my many private universities, limit your choices to seize your future meaningfully. I was able to attend the University of Kentucky without a penny of debt and view that as one of the best economic choices I made in my teens.

You want to find a life partner of similar values and work ethic during the time best suited to forging that partnership. Many people, even most, are not ready to commit to a 70 year relationship with another person before they have even notched 20. They want to explore a range of relationships ideally in a city with lots of young people.

Others, and I was definitely in that camp, realize that their life's work was going to demand a lot, and I mean a lot, of time working HARD. Ditto for your partner, who may be even working harder (as was my case). If you are the kind of person where work-life balance tilts heavily towards work, the University of Kentucky offers an excellent opportunity to front load dating. Several of my friends, all of whom work crazy hours, met their significant other at the University of Kentucky and all have strong partnership that enabled them to focus on the heavy work of career building. Among my current friends, it seems BYU has a similar culture of front-loading dating.

You feel the call to Agricultural Biotechnology or Equine Sciences and want to be where the best people in the world work on what you care about. Clusters of excellence are rare and one of the best ways to maximize your productivity as a student is to go to those clusters. There are very few places with as well developed an agricultural biotechnology program as the University of Kentucky, and if that is your path it is wise to excel in following it.

PS: My wife and several of her friends took their experience at Ag Biotech into excelling at some of the finest medical and law schools in America, so excellence can lead to further excellence.

You are a STEM nerd that wants some liberal arts before the rest of your STEM-heavy life. The Honors Program is something very special about the University of Kentucky. If you are the kind of kid who spent high school programming computers, and will spend the rest of life after college programming computers, you will absolutely benefit from the life-expanding view of the humanities. You will become a better person.

You want to build roots in Kentucky. For some people, emphatically not me, the idea of a middle class lifestyle in Lexington-Louisville-Northern Kentucky with two kids and a large, comfortable house appeals. If that resonates with you, UK could be a good choice.

Here are the worst reasons:

You want to get seriously rich in your lifetime (let's define that as top 0.1% of wealth) , the University of Kentucky will not significantly accelerate your path to riches. UK is not optimized to prepare the kind of wealth generation that more entrepreneurially focused universities (Stanford, Babson College) are. If you want to own your own high growth business, by far one of the best ways to generate wealth, UK lacks the industry links and alumni network necessary to become really, really rich.

You want to be surrounded by creative, committed peers that lead you to excel in your discipline. There are certain subjects (music, web design, entrepreneurship) where your ability to succeed is a strong factor of the quality of your peers. UK is not optimized to build critical mass of creative folks, and so struggles to build the kind of impressive community that MIT does in computer programming or IU does in music.

You want to leave Kentucky forever and never look back. There was a set of us who felt that Kentucky was not a place where we could be the fullest expression of our inner selves, where what makes us special was not valued. For those people, UK will mean building networks and connections with folks who will, on average, stay in Kentucky. Probably best to start with a clean slate in a place where you want to be established.
